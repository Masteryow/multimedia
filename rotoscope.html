1

Import the Reference Footage

Open After Effects and import the reference video clip into your project. Drag it onto the timeline to create a new composition based on the footage settings.

2

Plan Your Body Part Layers

Decide which body parts to isolate — head, torso, arms, legs, etc. Each part gets its own layer so they can be colored and animated independently.

3

Duplicate the Layer for Each Body Part

Select the footage layer in the timeline and duplicate it for each body part you plan to rotoscope. This gives every part its own isolated copy to work on.

💡 Rename each duplicated layer clearly (e.g. "Left Arm", "Head", "Torso") to keep your timeline organized before you start masking.
4

Pre-compose Each Body Part Layer

Select a body part layer and pre-compose it to keep the roto work isolated. Use the shortcut:

Ctrl + Shift + C
⌨️ In the Pre-compose dialog, choose "Move all attributes into the new composition" so your masks and effects stay inside the pre-comp.
5

Rotoscope Frame by Frame

Double-click into each pre-comp and use the Pen tool or Roto Brush to trace the body part outline on every frame. Adjust the mask path carefully to follow the movement.

7

Apply Flat Color to Each Body Part

Once a body part is masked, apply a solid color or Fill effect to paint that isolated layer with a flat color. This creates the signature stylized rotoscope look.

8

Repeat for All Body Parts

Go back to the main composition and repeat Steps 3–7 for every body part layer. Stack the pre-comps in the correct order (arms behind torso, etc.) to build the complete figure.

9

Review, Refine, and Export

Play through the full animation in the main composition. Adjust timing, layer order, and colors as needed, then export via Composition → Add to Adobe Media Encoder Queue as an MP4.
